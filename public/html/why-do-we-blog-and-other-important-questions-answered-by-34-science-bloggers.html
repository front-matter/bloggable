Reading these blog posts is not only interesting and entertaining, but probably also a very good introduction to the current state of science blogging. Below is a personal summary of some of the answers. Oh, the best title was probably from Frank Norman (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/U81B5C465/blog/2008/11/16/la-meme-chose?): La meme chose.

1. What is your blog about?

Most bloggers seem to write about many different science-related topics. And only a minority about the actual science they are doing. Some bloggers gave more specific answers:

  • My blog is about science, in particular evolution and genomes. (T. Ryan Gregory (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://genomicron.blogspot.com/2008/11/why-do-we-blog-and-other-important.html?))
  • It's a sort of a window of transparency over a weird scientific environment, the Italian science jobs and funding market. (Massimo Pinto (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/massimopinto/blog/2008/11/15/whats-this-bel-paese?))
  • Basically the philosophical implications of science. (John Wilkins (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/evolvingthoughts/2008/11/another_goldang_meme.php?))
  • Anything releted to Biotechnology in Frederick County, MD. (Jim Hardy (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://fredcobio.wordpress.com/2008/11/17/scientistist-their-desks?))
  • The general theme is how we can bring the worlds of information technology and the life sciences together. (Deepak Singh (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://mndoci.com/blog/2008/11/16/why-do-we-blog/?))
  • I am trying to put the people behind the science into the spotlight: the technicians, operational support, science management and others. (Steffi Suhr (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/stuffysour/blog/2008/11/14/its-the-need-to-communicate?))
  • We write about making sense of medicine and medical science in museums. (Thomas Soderqvist (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://www.corporeality.net/museion/2008/11/17/why-do-we-blog-and-other-important-questions-reply-to-martin-fenner-nature-networks/?))
  • Ostensibly about theoretical population biology. (Mike Fowler (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/mike/blog/2008/11/18/follow-those-lemmings-where?))
  • I am interested in how the internet is changing the way we publish and communicate science. (me (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/mfenner/blog/2008/11/14/some-answers-for-henry-gee?))

2. What will you never write about?

Several people mentioned that they would not give out personal information about other people, or comment directly on what's going on in their institution/company. Most people also avoid to talk religious beliefs or politics. Confidential information, including unreleased papers, was mentioned several times. The release of calcium from intracellular stores (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/henrygee/blog/2008/03/05/the-release-of-calcium-from-intracellular-stores-and-other-stuff?) is another topic that several people would never blog about. Also:

  • Only write about things I actually understand. (Ed Yong (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/notrocketscience/2008/11/why_blog_the_meme_returns.php?))
  • I will never ask anyone to give me money via this blog. (Maxine Clarke (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://petrona.typepad.com/petrona/2008/11/pinning-ones-hamster-to-the-mast.html?))
  • I hope that I will not have to write blog posts that are evaluated, measured and put on a resumé. (me (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/mfenner/blog/2008/11/14/some-answers-for-henry-gee?))

3. Have you ever considered leaving science?

Several people said something similar to Bora Zivkovic (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/clock/2008/11/the_science_blog_meme.php?): Leaving research – yes, I already did that. Leaving science – never.

4. What would you do instead?

Some interesting answers. And a science background would be helpful in most of the jobs:

  • Zookeeper, or an animal trainer in a circus (Bora Zivkovic (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/clock/2006/06/how_to_become_a_biologist.php?))

  • Own a used book store (John Dupuis (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://jdupuis.blogspot.com/2008/11/science-blog-meme-why-do-we-blog.html?))
  • I'd try my best to get a job in video game development (Andrew Perry (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://blog.pansapiens.com/2008/11/17/that-science-blog-meme-thing-going-around/?))
  • Playing football for England (Sally Church (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://www.pharmastrategyblog.com/2008/12/science-blog-meme.html?))

5. What do you think will science blogging be like in 5 years?

This was a difficult question that some didn't answer. Larry Moran (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://sandwalk.blogspot.com/2008/11/why-do-we-blog-and-other-important.html?) said: pretty much the same as it is now. T. Ryan Gregory (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://genomicron.blogspot.com/2008/11/why-do-we-blog-and-other-important.html?) thinks that more professional researchers will join the blogosphere as this becomes socially acceptable. Andrew Perry (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://blog.pansapiens.com/2008/11/17/that-science-blog-meme-thing-going-around/?) thinks that research groups will be tied together more and more by their blogs. Eva Amsen (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/U27CE62BB/blog/2008/11/14/blog-survey?) thinks that there will be so many science blogs that we have to specialize. And I wrote that some science bloggers will be able to make enough money to earn a living from it.

6. What is the most extraordinary thing that happened to you because of blogging?

Thomas Soderqvist (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://www.corporeality.net/museion/2008/11/17/why-do-we-blog-and-other-important-questions-reply-to-martin-fenner-nature-networks/?) said that there is no the most extraordinary thing. But I hadn't expected to get so many interesting contacts with colleagues around the world. Many people (including myself) had similar answers. Going to SciFoo (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://www.nature.com/nature/meetings/scifoo/index.html?) is certainly an extraordinary thing an SciFoo invitation was mentioned by Pedro Beltrao (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://pbeltrao.blogspot.com/2008/11/why-do-we-blog.html?), Jim Hardy (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://fredcobio.wordpress.com/2008/11/17/scientistist-their-desks?), Duncan Hull (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://duncan.hull.name/2008/11/17/science-blog-meme-why-do-we-blog/?) and Deepak Singh (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://mndoci.com/blog/2008/11/16/why-do-we-blog/?). Some other answers:

  • The Editor-in-Chief of Nature once told David Attenborough that he should read my blog. (Ed Yong (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/notrocketscience/2008/11/why_blog_the_meme_returns.php?))
  • Getting a job with PLoS in the comments of one of my posts. (Bora Zivkovic (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/clock/2008/11/the_science_blog_meme.php?))
  • Co-founded a network of science bloggers (The DNA Network) and been invited (and accepted!) to work at MIT. (Ricardo Vidal (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://my.biotechlife.net/2008/11/16/the-science-blog-meme/?))
  • Getting interviewed by Jon Udell. (Deepak Singh (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://mndoci.com/blog/2008/11/16/why-do-we-blog/?))
  • I loaned a power cable to a Nature editor. (Bob O'Hara (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/boboh/blog/2008/11/16/memetic-selections?))

7. Did you write a blog post or comment you later regretted?

For most people that was not a big issue. Ed Yong (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/notrocketscience/2008/11/why_blog_the_meme_returns.php?) regrets to have written nice things about studies that later turned out to be rubbish not so good.

8. When did you first learn about science blogging?

Many different answers. Nodalpoint (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://www.nodalpoint.org/?) was mentioned by several bloggers, including Duncan Hull (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://duncan.hull.name/2008/11/17/science-blog-meme-why-do-we-blog/?), Paolo Nuin (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://blindscientist.genedrift.org/2008/11/16/the-science-blog-meme/?) and Pedro Beltrao (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://pbeltrao.blogspot.com/2008/11/why-do-we-blog.html?). T. Ryan Gregory (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://genomicron.blogspot.com/2008/11/why-do-we-blog-and-other-important.html?) was introduced to science blogging by his graduate student. The most hilarious answer is from Henry Gee (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://network.nature.com/people/henrygee/blog/2008/11/14/that-martin-fenner-effect?) and involves a garage, an old washing-machine motor and heavier-than-air flight.

9. What do your colleagues at work say about your blogging?

The standard answer seems to be that most of them don't know or don't care. I would hope that in the future we will have more answers like the one from Bora Zivkovic (https://web.archive.org/web/20151003123037/http://scienceblogs.com/clock/2008/11/the_science_blog_meme.php?): That's what they are paying me for and I hope they are happy.

10. How the heck do you have time to blog and do research at the same time?

Most people blog in their spare time. I hope to see more daytime bloggers that blog as part of their science job in 5 years (this relates to questions #5, #8 and #9).
