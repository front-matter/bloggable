We are all familiar with digital object identifiers (DOIs) provided by CrossRef (https://web.archive.org/web/20120610122411/http://www.crossref.org/?) to identify (and link to) journal articles. Some of us are familiar with the DOIs issued by DataCite (https://web.archive.org/web/20120610122411/http://datacite.org/whatisdoi.html?) to link to datasets. But most of us don’t know that CrossRef is also providing component DOIs (https://web.archive.org/web/20120610122411/http://www.crossref.org/help/Content/Components.htm?) that can provide persistent links to a particular table or figure in a paper. Linking to component DOIs is straightforward, table 1 of the paper above is 10.1371/journal.pone.0006022.t001 (https://web.archive.org/web/20120610122411/http://dx.doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0006022.t001?). Unfortunately only about 300,000 (https://web.archive.org/web/20120610122411/http://www.niso.org/publications/isq/free/Feeney_DOIs_for_Journals_ISQ_v22no3.pdf?) of the more than 40 million CrossRef DOIs are compontent DOIs.  There are many good reasons to use a direct (and persistent) link to a specific part of a scholarly journal article. And this becomes an even more important issue once DOIs for datasets are more commonly cited.
