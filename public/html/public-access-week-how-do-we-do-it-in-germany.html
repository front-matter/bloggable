Starting this week, papers submitted from NIH-funded research have to be made publicly available (https://web.archive.org/web/20120611110754/http://publicaccess.nih.gov/policy.htm?) no later than 12 months after publication. But what is the current situation in Germany, especially mandatory Open Access?

The Berlin Declaration (https://web.archive.org/web/20120611110754/http://oa.mpg.de/openaccess-berlin/berlindeclaration.html?) from October 2003 was a strong statement of support for Open Access and was signed by all major research and funding organizations, including Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft (DFG), Max Planck Gesellschaft (MPG), Helmholtz-Gesellschaft, Fraunhofer-Gesellschaft and Leibniz-Gemeinschaft. In contrast to the new NIH public access policy (and the Welcome Trust (https://web.archive.org/web/20120611110754/http://www.wellcome.ac.uk/About-us/Policy/Spotlight-issues/Open-access/Policy/index.htm?) and Howard Hughes Medical Institute (https://web.archive.org/web/20120611110754/http://www.hhmi.org/about/research/journals/main?action=search?)), there is no mandatory Open Access in any of these organizations.

The German Publisher Springer, one of the largest STM (Science, Technology and Medicine) publishers, has a Springer Open Choice (https://web.archive.org/web/20120611110754/http://www.springer.com/open+choice?SGWID=0-40359-0-0-0?) option. Authors who pay for this option will retain the copyright of their paper and the article will be made available with full Open Access.
