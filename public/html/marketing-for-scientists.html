The April issue (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://www.nature.com/nmat/journal/v11/n4/index.html?) of Nature Materials contains three articles that discuss marketing strategies for scientists. The Editorial (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/nmat3300?) (“The scientific marketplace”) introduces the topic and explains why scientists should consider marketing their work. The issue also contains an interview (Martin, 2012) (“The m word”) with astrophysicist Marc Kuchner who published a book titled Marketing for Scientists (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://marketingforscientists.com/?). Finally, there is a commentary (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/nmat3283?) (“One-click science marketing”) by me discussing strategies and tools that scientists can use to promote their work. The commentary also includes links to pages by Phil Bourne (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://www.scivee.tv/user/4?) (SciVee), Jonathan Eisen (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://www.mendeley.com/profiles/jonathan-eisen?) (Mendeley), Rosie Redfield (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://rrresearch.fieldofscience.com/?) (blog), John Hawks (“John Hawks Weblog · John Hawks Weblog,” 2016) (blog) and Cameron Neylon (https://web.archive.org/web/20160528080410/http://cameronneylon.net/?) (personal webpage).

Scientists may feel uncomfortable about marketing their work, but we all are doing it already. We know that giving a presentation at a key meeting can be a boost for our career, and we know about the importance of maintaining an academic homepage listing our research interests and publications. And people reading this blog will understand that a science blog can be a powerful marketing tool.

Feel free to add your thoughts and suggestions in the comments.
